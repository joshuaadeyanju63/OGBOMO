Reduce, Reuse, Recycle

Most of what leaves an Ogbomosoland home isn't really "waste" — food scraps can go back into soil, bottles and scrap metal already have buyers, and a lot of what fills a heap could have been avoided in the first place.

Dínkù

Reduce

Choose refillable containers over single-use sachets and bags where you can, and buy only what your household will actually use.

Tún Lò

Reuse

Repair before replacing. Bottles, containers, and cloth sacks can usually serve a second or third purpose before they're truly waste.

Àtúntò

Recycle

Keep plastic, metal, glass, and paper separate from food waste so they can be processed or resold instead of buried in a dump.

Sorting at home

A 4-bin guide

This is separate from the Ilé / Ọjà / Kòtò collection streams the council uses for dispatch — this is what to separate inside your own compound before it ever reaches a route.

Organic

Food peels, leftovers, garden waste. Can be composted for kitchen gardens instead of collected at all.

Plastic

Bottles, sachets, containers — rinse and keep dry. This is the category buyers pay for.

Paper & Cardboard

Cartons, newspaper, packaging. Keep dry so it stays usable for recycling.

Glass & Metal

Cans, tins, scrap metal, glass bottles. Widely bought by local scrap dealers.

Waste can pay

This isn't hypothetical

In Ibadan — two hours from Ogbomoso — PETsPoint Recycling Nigeria already pays residents for clean plastic bottles, and national platforms like Scrapays let households sell recyclables digitally at fair prices. Many Ogbomoso residents already sell scrap metal and bottles informally to local buyers; nothing like this is yet formalized inside Ogbomosoland itself, and connecting that demand to sorted household waste is part of what this platform can grow into.
